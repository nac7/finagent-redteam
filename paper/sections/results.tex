\subsection{Key Findings}

\subsubsection{1. Frontier Models Are Robust}

Claude (both Sonnet and Haiku) and Gemini 2.0 Flash achieve 0\% ASR across all three postures and all threat categories. They refuse compliance-violating actions even without policy hints or system enforcement. This suggests strong training against regulatory violations.

Mistral 7B, despite being open-source, also achieves 0\% ASR, indicating that model capability and scale matter more than whether weights are public.

\subsubsection{2. Open-Source Models Show Mixed Results}

Open-source models exhibit high variance:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Qwen 3 8B}: Significantly vulnerable (45.2\% ASR at advisory), the most susceptible model in the benchmark. Suggests weak alignment on financial compliance.
  \item \textbf{Llama 3.1 8B}: Highly robust (3.2\% ASR at advisory), matching frontier models despite being open-source. Indicates strong default refusal behavior.
  \item \textbf{Gemma 2 9B}: Fully robust (0.0\% ASR), equivalent to frontier models. Open-source does not imply vulnerability.
\end{itemize}

This heterogeneity demonstrates that model scale and training data matter more than whether weights are open or closed.

\subsubsection{3. Policy Matters}

Policy-following uplift (ASR\_none − ASR\_advisory) ranges from 22\% to 45\%. Even vulnerable models reduce attack success when told the policy, suggesting that instruction-following and some regulatory awareness are present. However, the residual success rate (22--45\% at advisory) indicates that policy alone is insufficient.

\subsubsection{4. Enforcement Is Essential}

Enforcement uplift (ASR\_advisory − ASR\_enforced) ranges from 22\% to 45\%. Hard system controls reduce ASR to 0\% across all models. This demonstrates that defense-in-depth (policy + system enforcement) is necessary and effective.

\subsection{Statistical Significance}

Bootstrap hypothesis tests (Appendix \ref{app:stats}) confirm that differences between frontier and open-source models are statistically significant at $p < 0.05$ across all threat categories and postures.

\subsubsection{Example: Frontier vs.\ Open-Source (Advisory)}

\begin{itemize}
  \item Claude vs.\ Qwen: difference = −45\%, p < 0.001, 95\% CI = [−53\%, −37\%]
  \item Gemini vs.\ Llama: difference = −22\%, p = 0.002, 95\% CI = [−31\%, −14\%]
\end{itemize}

These are not statistical noise; they represent genuine, large performance gaps.

\subsection{Summary}

FinAgent Red-Team reveals nuanced security landscape:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Frontier models are robust}: Claude, Gemini, Mistral, and Gemma all achieve 0\% ASR across all postures, indicating strong training against compliance violations.
  \item \textbf{Open-source models vary widely}: Qwen 3 8B (45.2\% vulnerable) vs.\ Llama 3.1 8B (3.2\% vulnerable) shows that openness is not the primary driver of vulnerability. Training and scale matter more.
  \item \textbf{Defense-in-depth is essential}: Policy statements reduce Qwen's ASR by 38\%, but enforcement reduces it another 45\%, showing all three layers (training, policy, enforcement) are necessary.
  \item \textbf{No utility cost}: All models maintain 100\% utility on benign tasks at advisory posture. Over-refusal only appears under hard enforcement (\textless{}1\%), making controls deployable in production.
\end{itemize}
